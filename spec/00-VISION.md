# Vision — Why This Is Worth Doing v1.0

> **The question this document answers:** *why is this worth doing?* — nothing about *how*. The mechanics live in the Specification; this page holds only the reason and the direction.

---

People remember decisions through their **outcomes** — which makes learning from them dishonest. This system preserves *what was knowable*, records *what was chosen*, and measures *what actually happened*, so that future decisions can improve.

Everything below is an expansion of that one paragraph.

---

## The problem

A person **cannot reliably learn from their own decisions.** Three causes, and they compound:

1. **Memory.** The past is **reconstructed, not recorded** — remembered through how it turned out. You cannot honestly ask *"what did I actually know at the time?"*, so you cannot tell skill from luck.
2. **Emotion.** The moment of action is driven by FOMO, fear, and the last headline — not by a plan made calmly in advance.
3. **No scorecard.** Neither your own calls nor any tool's are measured against what really happened. Confidence moves on feeling, not evidence.

*Remember → Decide → Learn* is broken at every step. Fix the memory and the measurement, and the learning can finally be honest.

---

## What the system is

**A system that preserves decision history and helps people make better future decisions.** It keeps three things faithfully — what was knowable, what was chosen and why, and what actually happened — for **every** decision, whoever made it. *How* it does this is the rest of the Specification.

---

## What success looks like

The system succeeds when, for **any** past decision, it can answer all five honestly:

1. **What was knowable** at the moment of the decision?
2. **What did each party believe?**
3. **What was chosen** — including a deliberate *no-action*?
4. **What actually happened** afterward?
5. **Was the decision good** — *measured, not remembered*?

And across many decisions:

- **Strategies are comparable and improvable** — you can *show*, on evidence, that one is better.
- **Trust is earned.** Confidence in any decider rises only as its measured record justifies it.

---

## What it will never be

These are commitments at the level of **values**, not scope — they must hold in ten years as surely as today:

- **Not a black box.** Every conclusion is traceable to the evidence beneath it.
- **Not an oracle.** It measures what happened; it never claims to foretell what will.
- **Never a remover of human accountability.** Authority over a decision may one day be delegated — responsibility for it never is.

---

## The horizon

**Investing is the first domain** — the place the system must prove itself. But the model beneath it is not about markets: it is a general model of how a decision is made and learned from. So in time the same system could serve other decision domains, and any decider within it — human or machine — could earn *more* responsibility, **strictly and only** as its measured record justifies.

The world this points toward is one where **every decision that matters is explainable, replayable, and improvable.** And the rule that gets there without harm:

> **A decision system should earn trust before it earns authority.**
